| 3. Boss Pattern Commentary and Hints  |
+---------------------------------------+

This section is inspired by similar sections in the omakes of Touhou 6 ~ the Embodiment of
Scarlet Devil and fan game Touhou Elegant Impermanence of Sakura. It just contains some of
my musings and comments about the development of some of the spells and some hints if
you can't capture them.

○ Beginner Sign "My First Spellcard!" (Midboss Spell 1)

Despite the name, this spell card was the *second* one to be completed, though it was the
first to be added to the stage itself while I was developing and testing the
infrastructure for how bosses should manage their spell cards and attacks. During that
time, the placeholder attack was just random spam, aka Eternal Meek.

As for the pattern itself, given that it's Doremi's first spell card, it's pretty
simple. From the very beginning ideations of this project, I always knew that I wanted the
first midboss spell to be a survival as it would have to sync with the entirety of the
music's chorus. Eventually, I thought of using the rotating triplet of music notes, which
is a reference to the music notes that spin on top of the Kururu Poron (the second magical
staff the girls use in Season 1). To make the units feel more like a group and less
"bare", I wrapped them up in pellets, which are a bit evocative of Aya's nonspells.

Originally, there was no second phase, but I thought the first phase dragged on too long
to meet the required time, so I went back later and added a second phase, where the notes
lose the pellets, but are shot more quickly, and some aimed hearts are added in. For
beginners, it might seem too hard, but keep moving and the aimed hearts won't hit you. The
notes are fired in a regular pattern, so some sort of left-right-left-right movement will
work.

○ Natural Sign "Butterfly Smelling the Flowers" (Midboss Spell 2)

This was the first spell card to be completed. As with the first midboss spell, I knew
from the start that I wanted the second midboss spell to happen here in the stage and
music progression, which meant it also had to be a survival. This one was shorter, though,
so I could get away with only one pattern for all of it.

As for the pattern itself, I think it's something that new danmaku designers get an
appreciation for, which is how you can make nice patterns that look curvy and spinny
without actually doing that much work at all: you just make two (or more) points rotate
around some center, and shoot bullets towards that center. The boss adds some slow aimed
fans to keep the player on their toes while they're spinning as an easy second element
layered on top. I was particularly had Touhou 20 Nareko's Riddle Sign "Ancient Trap" in
mind which has a similar two-layer design.

Once you get enough practice, the spinning should become second nature, and you can
concentrate exclusively on avoiding the aimed fan.

○ Boss Non-spell 4 (Aiko)

This was the second boss attack to be completed, though I worked on it concurrently with
Aiko's second spell.

Because that spell involves a bouncing yin-yang orb bullet, I decided to introduce that
mechanic as a sort of "heads up!" in this nonspell. Originally, this nonspell was to be
Aiko's second nonspell, but I moved it up here to make room for another necessary gameplay
requirement in that nonspell (see below for details).

The pattern is a pretty basic loop of a spread of yin-yang orbs that bounce off the walls
once, then a ring of notes to dodge through. The difficulty of this ended up a bit too
high though.

○ Love Sign "Star Spiral" (Aiko)

This was the first attack of the boss fight to be completed. Aiko's straightforward and
unabashed nature reminded me a lot of Marisa, so the usage of star bullets also carried
forward into her attack design.

Coming up with ideas was a bit tough for the first boss fight pattern. The two midboss
spells came pretty naturally as they were timeouts and thus a bit more "showy" than
actually strictly needing to be valuable in a gameplay manner.

Eventually, I landed on having Aiko set up these spirals, then have them disintegrate
while Aiko fires aimed fans (another example of the simple two-layered approach).

The spiral positions used to be nearly completely random horizontally which made for some
impossible situations if the arms overlapped weirdly. Eventually, I made the first two
spirals fixed, and only the third one was allowed to vary. To dodge it, I recommend moving
away horizontally from the third spiral and keeping your vision wide to make sure no stray
bullets from the other spirals blindside you.

○ Boss Non-spell 5 (Group)

When I first was deciding how many attacks I wanted to have, I settled on having two group
and individual spell cards for each girl, plus the survival and the final spell as a
trio. The first four spells would be more orthodox patterns focused on the dodging, while
the next four spells would be wackier, more gimmicky attacks befitting an Extra-like
stage, then the survival and final spells would round it back out a bit as the closers.

This was the fifth boss attack to be completed. I knew I wanted the trio gimmick spell to
focus on the girls' fairy familiars, so I thought I'd introduce them in the prior
nonspell.

Coming up with and coding this attack was a surprisingly smooth process. I just
implementing the first thing that came to mind for all three fairies and it ended up
fitting together super well.

The attack patterns are very tangentially based on each girl's shot type in Ojamajo Doremi
SHOOT, a Ojamajo Doremi fan shmup from the 2000's and the first STG I ever played. In it,
Hazuki has a homing shot, Doremi has a 5 way shot, and Aiko has a direct, forward-focus
shot. Each of the fairies' patterns vaguely reflects their owner's shot in that game.

Hint: Mimi (rightmost) has the most threatening attack, so concentrate on attacking her
over the other fairies (sorry Mimi).

○ Witch Sign "Fairy Kaleidoscope" (Group)

This was the eight boss attack to be completed. From the start, I decided to have this
attack be cast primarily by the fairy familiars, so the bosses themselves were set to
wander in a ring around the outside, firing decoration bullets.

I mulled over the movement pattern of the inner fairies for a while. For a while, they
simply circled continuosly in the opposite direction of the bosses, with lasers creating
an enclosing triangle. That felt a bit boring and lacking in dynamism.

Eventually, I settled on the little rotation movement in the final version (which involved
a side diversion to learn how CSS's cubic-bezier function works and how to implement my
own function, which required numerical methods like Newton's Method. Educational!). Each
fairy's wave has their own fixed pattern that I just kind of came up with on the fly.

Not too gimmicky of an attack compared to all the following gimmick attacks, but it's fun
to dodge each phase as it comes, especially Dodo's spinning phase.

Hints:

1. Mimi's phase (bullets bouncing off of your aura) has two ways to dodge it. One is
easier for survival, while the other maximizes graze. Try figuring out both!
2. Rere's phase (bubble bullet charging up at center) is a bit hard. I wonder if there's
room somewhere to slip in and hide...

○ Boss Non-spell 7 (Hazuki)

This was the seventh boss attack to be completed. In keeping with the design philosophy of
introducing or hinting at a spell's gimmick in the preceding nonspell, I decided to
introduce some wisps in this nonspell.

Keen-eyed players will notice this attack quickly ended up as a clone of Koakuma's Stage 5
midboss spell in Touhou Fantastic Danmaku Festival 1. I briefly experimented with
replacing the orbit bullets with other bullet types like butterflies, but nothing seemed
to fit, so I used small glow orbs, like FDF1 does.

However, I added a little twist inspired by Touhou Elegant Impermanence of Sakura: If you
kill the wisps at the right time, they'll drop large green items when they cancel the
rings around them. They provide quite a bit of Point Item Value (up to 16k per wisp if you
time it just right), so it can be a fun challenge to dart around and kill them all with
the right timing. You can get tons of item value from this one nonspell.

Hazuki also has a damage shield, so you have to kill the wisps to bring her health
down. Of course, if you just want to survive, just hold down the Shot button to cancel the
bullets. But playing for score is fun, try it out!

I was quite satisfied with how I managed to make an attack inspired by two other
influential fangames, but put together with a nice twist.

○ Paranormal Sign "Hazuki's Ghostbusting Challenge" (Hazuki)

This was the sixth boss attack to be completed. Originally, I wanted Hazuki's gimmick
spell to be based on the repeated gag in the series of her being scared by ghosts and
ghost stories. I was envisioning something based on Touhou Categorical Imperative Valid?'s
friendly fire system, specifically the stage 2 boss's final spell. In that spell, enemies
appear and continually attack the boss, and the player is encouraged to "save" the boss by
killing all the enemies that appear, instead of shooting down the boss themselves.

After some thought, however, I thought it would be unfair to Hazuki to portray her in that
sort of timid light, so I reworked as a "trial of guts" of sorts, in which she'd summon
the ghosts and you'd have to defeat them to capture the spell.

As implied, you should focus on killing all the wisps. The wisps have a pool of three
possible revenge attacks spawned when they die. If you take too long to shoot the wisps
down, Hazuki will do the ghostbusting herself. As a minor punishment in that case, the
revenge patterns will become harder (plus, the ghostbusting attack from Hazuki also spawns
extra bullets on screen that get in the way).

You'll get an extra bonus if you capture the spell by killing every single wisp yourself,
without Hazuki killing one for you.

○ Boss Non-spell 8 (Aiko)

This was the third boss attack to be completed, but it was also worked on concurrently
with the next spell card. Originally, non-spell 4 was in this position, but I moved it out
to make room for another design. Basically, the next spell card requires the player to
start at a specific position on screen, so I decided to use some smart gameplay design
(tm) to move the player into that spot so they're already there by the time the spell
starts.

In keeping with the Extra tradition of final non-spells being super easy, the attack
itself is quite simple, Aiko just narrows some lasers Moonlight Ray-style while she moves
down the screen, forcing you into the aforementioned position. While this happens, the
lasers spawn aimed stars where they hit the sides of the screen. It's just easy streaming,
but it's probably a blind-killer if you're seeing it for the first time, since there's not
much room to maneuver if you aren't streaming properly. Implementing the "where do the
lasers hit the sides of the screen" logic took me back to my Algebra days in high school
(finally, a use for point-slope form linear equations).

Once Aiko reaches the bottom of the screen, she switches to some rapidly aimed heart fans
to continue applying pressure to the player to stay at the bottom of the screen. If you
keep insisting on going too far up, she starts shooting self-defense kunais to warn you to
not do that. I also made her do shoot the kunais when you try to time the nonspell out,
just for fun. The kunais are randomly either aimed at you or aimed away from you just
annoyingly enough to be a bit of a challenge for those wanting to time out every pattern.

○ Athletic Sign "Aiko's Pinball Penalty Shootout" (Aiko)

This was the fourth boss attack to be completed. From the start, I knew I wanted Aiko's
"gimmick spell" to be related to her status as the tomboyish athletic girl of the
series. I briefly considered having two goals on opposite sides of the field (you know,
like a proper soccer/hockey field...) and making it a sort of "air hockey" round of sorts,
but tabled it because I was lazy and didn't want to have to write a pong/air hockey AI.

I settled on it being a football penalty shootout, where you have to defend the goal
against Aiko's penalty kicks, with the punishment for a goal being death (how dark), but I
wasn't sure what would happen to the ball if you successfully blocked the kick. It seemed
a bit too boring for Aiko to just catch the ball and kick again.

I decided to make it a bit chaotic by giving the ball a pretty large velocity, having it
bounce off the player and walls, and maintain most of its velocity when bouncing. It
became a lot more reminiscent of pinball, hence the spell name.

Implementing the bouncing physics took quite a bit of finagling and tweaking and the
resulting code would probably make my Physical Simulation professor quite sad (sorry
Dr. Vouga), but it was the cleanest I could get without spending a ton of time either
binding to a physics library or writing a lot more code to make it more accurate. There's
some rare edge cases where the ball will get stuck in the wall and produce a ton of stars,
but I haven't managed to figure out why that happens, so if it does, just accept it with a
smile and press the Bomb key ^^;

This spell is quite chaotic at first and will be pretty challenging for less experienced
players. Some hints:

1. Aiko has three places she will attempt to kick the ball, depending on your position
within the penalty box. You can learn when each happens and use that to your benefit:
  a. Directly down the center towards the goal
  b. Towards the left or right sides of the goal
  c. Doing an air hockey bounce move off one of the side walls

2. The stars produced when the ball hits the walls are predictable. When high on the
screen, the stars are randomly aimed. When low on the screen, the stars are completely
aimed around you. Try to ignore the stars when the ball is bouncing around low on screen,
they're mostly there to distract you.

3. In a bit of a Sonic Spinball moment, Aiko is damaged when the ball hits her (don't
think too hard about why she would design a spell like that...). You *can* still capture
the spell by shooting her the whole time, but it'll go a lot faster if you can manipulate
the ball into hitting her. You also get an additional bonus if the spell is captured using
the ball as the finishing blow.

4. The direction the ball is reflected when bouncing of the player is simplified to the 8
cardinal and ordinal directions when you're moving while you collide with the ball. You
can actually use this, in combination with hints 1 and 3, to direct the ball somewhere
convenient each kick. If the ball hits you when you're not moving, it just bounces
directly to the normal vector of you and the ball (which isn't physically accurate, but
don't dwell on that too much).

5. Don't worry about running into Aiko when she comes to collect the ball. During that
time, she has no boss collision.

6. When in doubt, bomb. The ball keeps bouncing around the field for quite a while, so you
can get in some damage after your bomb ends while it's still bouncing around on the
screen, before the next kick comes.

7. If you're looking for extra challenge, try grazing the lasers of the field as much as
you can while you're dealing with dodging stars and kicking the ball. It's pretty tough!
